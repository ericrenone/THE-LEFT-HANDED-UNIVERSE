---

## 1. THE CENTRAL CLAIM

A single structural pattern — what we call here the **invariant geometry of erasure** — recurs across radically different historical epochs, disciplines, and political systems: the contributions of women of exceptional mastery are systematically made invisible, appropriated, or deprioritized in favor of male peers by the institutions that govern recognition. The three figures examined here — the fictional swordswoman Pei Shi'er (*Chang'an*, 2023), the mathematician Dr. Maryam Mirzakhani (1977–2017), and the nuclear physicist Dr. Chien-Shiung Wu (1912–1997) — do not share a century, a continent, a discipline, or a culture. What they share is the invariant. The geometry does not change. Only its surface coordinates do.

This analysis draws on intersectional feminist historiography, the sociology of cultural capital, and philosophy of science to dissect that geometry — and to read the three women's different strategies of resistance against it. It incorporates archival discoveries made public in 2026, following the deaths of the last surviving 1957 Nobel laureates, which now permit, for the first time, a documented reckoning with Wu's omission from the Nobel Prize in Physics.

## 2. THREE FIGURES, THREE DISCIPLINES, ONE STRUCTURAL PROBLEM

### 2.1 Pei Shi'er — *Chang'an* (长安三万里, 2023)

Pei Shi'er appears in the 2023 Chinese animated feature film *Chang'an* (also known internationally as *30,000 Miles from Chang'an*), directed by **Xie Junwei and Zou Jing**, produced by Light Chaser Animation Studios. The film is set during the Tang Dynasty (618–907 CE) and follows the decades-long friendship of the poets Li Bai and Gao Shi. Pei Shi'er is a secondary figure whose function is nonetheless structurally central: she disguises herself as a man ("Young Master Pei") and surpasses her male peers — including Gao Shi — in martial ability, only to find that neither her identity nor her talent earns her institutional standing. The imperial order has no commission for a woman, regardless of demonstrated mastery.

Her story is not embellishment. It is the most faithful representation of a historical fact: Tang Dynasty women of exceptional capability — Li Ye, Xue Tao, countless unnamed sword practitioners — operated in a system that could receive their excellence only under the erasure of their sex. The film renders this structurally rather than sentimentally: Pei Shi'er's triumph is private; her erasure is public and permanent.

### 2.2 Dr. Maryam Mirzakhani (1977–2017)

Born in Tehran, Iran, on May 3, 1977, Mirzakhani won gold medals at the International Mathematical Olympiad in 1994 and 1995 — attaining a perfect score in the latter — before earning her B.Sc. from Sharif University of Technology and her Ph.D. from Harvard University in 2004 under the supervision of Fields Medalist Curtis McMullen. Her dissertation on the volumes of moduli spaces of curves on hyperbolic surfaces was immediately recognized as extraordinary.

In 2014, at the International Congress of Mathematicians in Seoul, Mirzakhani was awarded the **Fields Medal** — the highest honor in mathematics, first awarded in 1936. She was **the first woman in the prize's 78-year history** to receive it, and the first Iranian. Her work on the dynamics and geometry of Riemann surfaces and their moduli spaces was described by the International Mathematical Union as demonstrating "a rare combination of superb technical ability, bold ambition, far-reaching vision, and deep curiosity." A second woman, the Ukrainian mathematician Maryna Viazovska, won the Fields Medal in 2022 — five years after Mirzakhani's death from metastatic breast cancer on July 14, 2017.

Mirzakhani's working style — large sheets of paper spread across the floor, drawing and redrawing geometric intuitions — was widely noted as an anomaly in a field that prizes formalized, sequential proof. She called mathematics "painting." This is not ornamental description. Her approach constituted a genuine epistemological challenge to the male-coded norm of deductive formalism. The feminization of her method was simultaneously its brilliance and the source of institutional hesitation about it. May 12, her birthday, is now celebrated globally as *Women in Mathematics Day*.

The moduli space $\mathcal{M}_{g,n}$ over which much of her research ranged can be understood informally as a space whose points each represent a distinct Riemann surface of genus $g$ with $n$ marked points — an infinite-dimensional space of curved surfaces. That the structure of this space should be studied by a woman who was the first of her sex to be recognized by the mathematics establishment is not a coincidence to be filed under symbolism. It is an irony that demands structural analysis.

### 2.3 Dr. Chien-Shiung Wu (1912–1997)

Born on May 31, 1912, in Liuhe, Jiangsu province, China, Wu studied physics at National Central University in Nanjing, emigrated to the United States in 1936, and completed her Ph.D. at the University of California, Berkeley, in 1940. She joined Columbia University's Manhattan Project contributions in 1944 and remained at Columbia for the rest of her career.

In 1956, theoretical physicists Tsung-Dao Lee and Chen-Ning Yang proposed that parity conservation — the principle that any physical process and its mirror reflection should behave identically — might not hold in weak nuclear interactions. They proposed experimental tests. **Wu designed and executed the decisive experiment.** Working at ultracold temperatures with cobalt-60, she confirmed parity violation in radioactive beta decay. On January 15, 1957, Columbia University held a press conference announcing the overthrow of a principle physicists had treated as inviolable. The *New York Times* ran a front-page headline the following morning.

In 1957, Lee and Yang received the Nobel Prize in Physics. Wu did not.

She went on to receive the inaugural Wolf Prize in Physics in 1978 and served as the first female president of the American Physical Society in 1975. Her book *Beta Decay* (1965) remains a standard reference for nuclear physicists. She died on February 16, 1997.

Three observations emerge from this geometry:

**First:** The institutional form of exclusion changes with historical context, but its function does not. Feudal military commissions, century-old prize committees, and Nobel selection panels all accomplish the same operation: the devaluation of female contribution at the precise moment when recognition would redistribute institutional authority.

**Second:** The disciplines themselves encode the problem differently. Mathematics prizes individual brilliance — which meant Mirzakhani's achievement was harder to appropriate. Experimental physics is inherently collaborative — which meant Wu's contribution was easier to disaggregate from the theory it confirmed, then route the Nobel toward the theorists. Tang Dynasty martial arts was structurally tied to military commission — which meant Pei Shi'er's excellence had no legitimate institutional pathway whatsoever.

**Third:** Resistance strategy tracks historical possibility. Where there are no institutions to contest, disguise is the only option (Pei Shi'er). Where institutions are penetrable but fragile, subversion through excellence and patience becomes viable (Mirzakhani). Where institutions are partially accessible but actively hostile, confrontation is available — though not always effective in the short term (Wu). The evolution of strategy is not moral progress; it is a function of structural aperture.

## 4. THREE STRATEGIES OF RESISTANCE

| STRATEGY | FIGURE | MECHANISM | STRUCTURAL CONDITION | OUTCOME |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Disguise** | Pei Shi'er | Perform masculinity to access space; reveal identity only after victory | No institutional recourse; zero aperture | Personal validation, public erasure |
| **Subversion through excellence** | Mirzakhani | Work outside male-coded deductive norm; redefine what mastery looks like | Institutional aperture exists; resistance to occupancy | Institutional rupture; global symbolic shift |
| **Direct confrontation** | Wu | Name discrimination publicly; advocate for systemic change; build record | Institutional access exists but excludes at apex | Mixed: recognized below Nobel threshold; normatively vindicated posthumously |

The geometry underlying all three is the same: a woman of exceptional mastery encounters a system that has not budgeted for her. The strategies diverge because the systems differ in permeability. None of the strategies is individually sufficient to dissolve the structure — but together they constitute the record.

## 5. THE ARCHIVE UNLOCKED: SOTA 2024–2026

The most significant development in Wu scholarship since the writing of the original document on which this analysis builds is archival.

The Nobel Prize archives are sealed while laureates remain alive. Tsung-Dao Lee and Chen-Ning Yang, who shared the 1957 Nobel Prize in Physics at ages 30 and 34 respectively, both lived into exceptional old age. Lee died in August 2024 aged 97, and Yang died on October 18, 2025 at the age of 103. Their deaths unlocked the 1957 archive.

An examination of the newly released Nobel archive from 1957 indicates that Wu was not nominated for that year's prize. Over the following 16 years, she received a total of 23 nominations — the last open archive at the time of current writing being 1974.

This is a finding of considerable significance. Wu was not merely overlooked in 1957; she was systematically nominated by her peers over and over across nearly two decades, and the prize was never awarded to her. The 23-nomination figure is not a footnote. It is a quantitative record of what the scientific community believed she had earned, set against the institutional record of what she received. The gap between those two registers is the geometry of erasure made numerical.

The opening of these archives does not settle every question. The Nobel Committee's deliberations remain sealed under a 50-year rule, meaning assessors' reasoning for 1957 will not be available until 2007 archival materials open. But the nomination record alone establishes: the problem was not that Wu was unrecognized by her peers. The problem was institutional, not epistemic.

## 7. FALSIFIABLE CLAIMS

The following claims derived from this analysis are in principle refutable by archival, quantitative, or historiographical evidence:

1. **Claim:** If the 1957 Nobel Committee's deliberative archive opens (available after 2007 under the 50-year rule), it will show that Wu's exclusion was discussed explicitly and resolved in favor of the theoretical over the experimental contribution — not simply omitted from consideration.

   *Refutation condition:* Archive reveals Wu was never discussed in deliberations, suggesting omission rather than active exclusion.

2. **Claim:** The rate of female Fields Medal winners will reach parity with male winners within four award cycles of Mirzakhani's 2014 breakthrough, given the documented pipeline expansion at the post-2014 junior-faculty level.

   *Refutation condition:* No additional female winners through 2030 award cycle, suggesting the 2014 rupture was nominal rather than structural.

3. **Claim:** In experimental physics, female contributors to collaborative Nobel-winning work are structurally more likely to be excluded from the prize than female theorists, because the collaborative nature of experimental work creates conditions for disaggregation of contributions.

   *Refutation condition:* Historical audit shows no statistically significant difference in exclusion rates between experimental and theoretical female contributors at Nobel level.

4. **Claim:** Fictional representations of women in male-dominated disciplines — such as Pei Shi'er in *Chang'an* — produce measurable attitudinal shifts in audiences toward gender equity in STEM when studied experimentally in educational contexts.

   *Refutation condition:* Controlled studies show no significant difference in STEM gender-equity attitudes between audiences exposed and unexposed to such narratives.
